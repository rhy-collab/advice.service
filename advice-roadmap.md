# Advice — Build Roadmap (SUPERSEDED — renamed to "Charter Consultancy", see charter-consultancy-roadmap.md for the live version)

> Working name: **Advice**. Built on the Charter Law codebase — same stack, same backbone, generalized from one vertical (legal) to a marketplace of advisory domains, with a debate-based advisor mechanism (the "board") layered on top instead of a single matched expert.
> Owner: Rhys — solo founder, building with Claude.
> Status: **PIVOTING** — engineering foundation from Charter Law carries over almost entirely. This document describes what changes and what's reused.
> Companion docs: `charter-law-roadmap.md` (previous plan — the legal vertical's Phase 0–5 detail still applies to the legal expert type specifically), `charter-law-fork-agent-super-prompt.md` (engineering ground truth as of 18 July 2026), `charter-law-tech-stack.md`.

## 0. What we are building (the one-paragraph version)

Advice is a marketplace of AI advisors across the problems every early-stage founder actually has — pricing, fundraising, go-to-market, pitch, legal, where to build, and developer/engineering — entered through one text box on the landing page. A founder describes their problem once; the system doesn't hand them a single confident answer, it convenes a small board of advisors with genuinely opposed points of view who independently stake out positions against real evidence, cross-examine each other once, and hand back a verdict that names its own assumptions and preserves disagreement instead of averaging it away. That entire process — context gathering, triage, the domain expert panel, and an unlimited ongoing chat with a synthesized "perfect agent" — is free. For legal specifically, the panel's process also feeds into the contract-review pipeline already built for Charter Law — template selection, AI-drafted redline, attorney sign-off, download, still gated behind a paid engagement with a real attorney. The one paid moment in the whole product, across every domain, is when a founder asks to be matched with a real individual — a working professional in that domain, not a titled role — priced off the actual problem, not a generic menu.

**The tech stack does not change — this is binding, not aspirational.** Advice stays on the exact architecture General Legal / Charter Law already runs: FastAPI + Postgres backend, Clerk auth, Stripe billing, GCS storage, React/Vite frontend, Anthropic Claude as the only model layer, Cloud Run hosting. Every round of the board (§1, §3) is new tables, new async orchestration, and new prompt content on top of that stack — not a new framework, not a new vendor, not a second model provider. If a future build session is ever tempted to reach for something outside this list (a vector DB, a different LLM provider, a new frontend framework) because a reference build used it, the answer is no — reimplement the same *effect* with what's already here, the way evidence retrieval (§3, item 4) is deliberately kept to a Postgres full-text step instead of adopting new retrieval infrastructure.

## 1. The user flow (what changes on the surface)

This is the concrete flow to build toward, in order:

1. **Landing page, text box front and center.** No sign-up wall first. The founder types their problem directly on the homepage.
2. **Frictionless sign-in on submit.** The moment they submit, they're prompted to sign in with Google (Clerk) — this is the only gate, and it happens after they've already committed by typing, not before. This creates their account and their first problem thread in the same motion.
3. **Straight into the app.** No separate "your matter is being processed" holding page — the founder lands directly in the same portal shell already built for Charter Law (matter list → problem/thread list), and sees their board convene live.
4. **Round 1 — Context.** The system captures the basics needed to ground everything downstream: how many users/customers, budget, funding stage and how far along they are, who the customer actually is, team size, goals. This is the same data as the persistent business-context profile (§4 Phase 4) — the first thread creates it, every later thread updates or reuses it rather than starting cold. Round 1 does still involve a debate, but on a narrower question than routing: a small board looks at what's been collected and argues out **"do we fully understand this startup?"** — is there enough here to reason about it responsibly, or are there real gaps (e.g. no clear revenue number, no stated funding stage, an ambiguous customer). If the board isn't satisfied, it asks the founder targeted follow-up questions before anything moves to Round 2 — it does not proceed on a thin profile just to keep the flow moving.
5. **Round 2 — Problem definition & triage.** With real context in hand (not just the founder's raw text), a small board decides two things: what the actual problem is — sharpened and clarified from however the founder first described it — and which single domain owns it, from a fixed menu of seven: **pricing strategy, fundraising, go-to-market, pitch & messaging, legal & compliance, resources & location, and developer/engineering** (build vs. buy, architecture, tech stack, hiring engineers, technical roadmap — the seventh domain, added alongside the original six). This is a routing decision, made visibly rather than as a silent classifier, but it is not yet the full opposed-priors debate — that's Round 3's job.
6. **Round 3 — Domain board.** Once a domain is fixed, a panel of roughly four domain experts with genuinely opposed priors and specialties is spawned within it (the pricing example — Value Pricer, PLG Advocate, Unit-Economics Hawk, plus a fourth lens — is the reference size; the developer domain's equivalent might be a Build-Fast/Ship-It advocate vs. a Scalability/Tech-Debt hawk vs. a Buy-Don't-Build pragmatist, plus a security/compliance lens). This is where the real board mechanism runs: independent positions drafted against evidence, one round of cross-examination, and a Chair ruling that preserves dissent, names its assumptions, and includes a validation plan — checked the whole way against the Round 1 context. **Round 3 is also where the budget for this specific problem gets estimated internally** — the panel's verdict includes an internal, McKinsey-team-bundle-style estimate of what solving this would actually cost in real expert time, sized to what this particular problem warrants, not the founder's stated budget repeated back. That estimate stays internal at this stage — nothing is charged yet. The panel also builds the actual artifact for that domain:
   - **Legal** — lawyer personas debate which contract template and stance fits the situation. That decision selects a template and playbook, which feeds directly into the existing Charter Law pipeline: AI drafts the first-pass redline against that template, the attorney reviews and approves, and the founder downloads a real tracked-changes `.docx`. The attorney-approval gate is unchanged and non-negotiable here — the panel's output is preparation, not the deliverable, exactly as before.
   - **GTM, fundraising, pitch, ecosystem, and developer/engineering** — the domain expert panel builds the actual plan against context, the same way the legal panel builds a contract: a **template** is selected (a channel/launch-plan template, a fundraising target list and process template, a pitch-deck and messaging-test template, a location/ecosystem shortlist template, a technical architecture/build-plan template for developer problems), a first-pass draft is produced against it, and a **review pass** (the domain's equivalent of a redline) marks it up before it's handed back to the founder. Legal is simply the domain where that review pass legally has to be a barred attorney — the other domains reuse the same template → draft → review → deliver shape without that specific gate.
7. **Stage 4 — two tabs: the free AI agent, and the paid Adviser tab.** Once Round 3's four experts reach their ruling, the system selects or generates a single best-fit persona, and that persona becomes the thread's default AI advisor, sitting front and center in the thread. **This AI chat stays free with no limit** — the entire journey from the landing-page text box through an unlimited ongoing conversation with the perfect agent costs the founder nothing. Sitting alongside it is a second tab, literally labeled **Adviser** — click it and the founder sees a small ranked range of real **individuals** on the platform matched to this exact problem. No titled role, no "Legal Lead" / "Dev Lead" branding — just real people with a track record in the relevant domain. Advice never prices this itself: when an individual joins the platform, they set two things — their own **hourly rate**, and a **skills profile** (what they're good at, and why, in their own words, not just a category tag). The system generates each individual's quote by combining that rate with Round 3's context-and-problem-specific real-hours estimate, then matches and ranks the resulting range of individuals for the founder to choose from — not a single suggestion. **Launch market: San Francisco** — the pool starts concentrated in one metro so there's real matching liquidity from day one, before expanding geographically. Advice takes a flat **10% platform fee** at launch (with room to move within a 10–20% band later, but starting simple at 10%) on top of whatever the individual already charges. **Once matched and booked, that individual gets their own little workbench** — the same generalized attorney-workbench surface Charter Law already built, pre-loaded with everything the free AI process already produced: the Round 1 context profile, the Round 2 triage reasoning, and the Round 3 panel's full debate, verdict, and draft artifact. They're reviewing and refining assembled work, not starting cold, which is also what keeps the real-hours estimate low enough to be worth quoting. All of this is built on the same tech stack as everything else in this document — no exception for the marketplace/workbench pieces.
8. **Parallel threads.** A founder can open as many problem threads as they want — pricing this week, fundraising next week — each running its own Round 1–4 independently, while the persistent business-context profile from Round 1 is shared across all of them, so later threads can skip straight to Round 2 if nothing material has changed.

## 2. What's already built and reused as-is

Everything under this heading is real, working code today (per `charter-law-fork-agent-super-prompt.md` §3) and needs generalizing, not rebuilding:

- **The lifecycle state machine** (`matter_service.py`, `LEGAL_TRANSITIONS`) — becomes the lifecycle for any problem thread, legal or not: `intake → ai_review → [board / attorney_queue] → review → delivered → completed`. Same enforced-transition, audit-trail pattern.
- **The approval gate** (`require_attorney_context`, the `attorney_approved` block on `delivered`) — stays exactly as-is, but now scoped specifically to the legal domain rather than applied globally. Other domains don't need it because a board verdict with preserved dissent isn't advice presented as certainty — it's already showing its own uncertainty, which is a different compliance posture than a single confident AI answer.
- **The playbook system** (`playbook_service.py`, `models/playbook.py`, per-client overlays) — becomes the home for advisor personas: instead of one playbook per contract type, each domain gets 2–3 opposed playbooks (one per advisor persona), each with its own fixed lens, rubric, and fallback guidance.
- **Auth, payments, storage** (Clerk, Stripe, GCS) — unchanged. Stripe billing extends to cover the human-expert-escalation budget, but that's a new price/product, not new plumbing.
- **The document pipeline** (`document_checker.py` read side, and Priority 2's tracked-changes `.docx` generation) — unchanged, and still the single highest-leverage piece of unbuilt work for the legal branch specifically.
- **The customer portal frontend** (`PortalPage.tsx`, `AttorneyWorkbenchPage.tsx`) — becomes the thread list and board view; the attorney workbench becomes one of several expert-facing surfaces.

## 3. What's genuinely new

Six additive pieces, all built with the existing stack rather than new tooling:

1. **Founder context profile** — a new table capturing what Round 1 collects: users/customers, budget, funding stage, traction, customer profile, team size, goals. Created on a founder's first thread, read and updated (not re-asked from scratch) on every later one. This is the thing Phase 8's "shared context" was describing — it now needs to exist from the first Round 1 build, not as a late add-on.
2. **Board data model** — new tables layered on the existing schema: a `Board` (one per Round 1 context-sufficiency check, Round 2 triage decision, or Round 3 domain panel — a `round` field distinguishes them), `AdvisorPosition` rows (one per advisor per round — Round 1's is a lighter completeness check against the context profile, Round 2 is a routing decision, Round 3 is the full independent-draft-plus-cross-examination debate), and a `Verdict` (the ruling for that round — for Round 1, a boolean-plus-follow-up-questions verdict rather than a full assumptions/dissent verdict, which is reserved for Round 3). Every `Board` carries a `domain` field drawn from the fixed seven-value enum (pricing, fundraising, GTM, pitch, legal, ecosystem, developer/engineering), set once in Round 2 and read by Round 3. **Round 3's `Verdict` also carries an internal `price_tier` enum (simple / standard / negotiation / drafting) and an `estimated_cost` field** — classified per the reactive-vs-originative, bounded-vs-open-ended logic in §6, not a raw hours estimate fuzzily mapped to a number. Set by the panel, used only to price the Stage 4 job quote when the founder asks for one, and never shown to the founder until they've actually requested to be matched with an individual. Additive Alembic migrations, same pattern as the existing 12.
3. **Parallel advisor orchestration** — running roughly four Claude calls concurrently with distinct system prompts (the opposed domain-expert personas) and no shared context until the cross-examination step, then a final Chair call that synthesizes. This is async FastAPI work, not a new framework — the discipline that matters is keeping the positions genuinely independent on the first pass so the disagreement is structural, not the same prompt asked three ways.
4. **Evidence retrieval for the board** — pulling live, relevant evidence (competitor pricing pages, market data) into a Round 3 panel's context so positions are grounded rather than generic. Keep this as simple as possible on this stack: a fetch-and-store step into Postgres with basic full-text search is enough to start; this does not require adopting new infrastructure just because a reference build used a particular retrieval library — the goal (grounded evidence, not model memory) is what matters, not the specific tool.
5. **The generalized artifact pipeline (Round 3)** — the legal document pipeline (template → AI first draft → review pass → deliverable) is the *reference implementation* of a pattern every domain now needs. Generalize `deliverable_service.py`'s eventual real output so each domain has its own `Template` type (contract templates for legal; a GTM plan template, a fundraising target-list-and-process template, a pitch-deck-and-message-test template, a location/ecosystem-shortlist template, a technical architecture/build-plan template for developer problems) and its own review-pass artifact. Legal's review pass is a barred attorney and a real tracked-changes `.docx`; the other domains' review pass is a lighter AI-plus-optional-human markup of the same drafted artifact — same shape, different rigor.
6. **The perfect-agent (free tab) + individuals directory (paid tab)** — after Round 3, synthesize a single persona (the "perfect agent") from the panel's output to be the thread's default AI advisor going forward, free and unlimited, living in its own tab. Separately, build a bespoke, hourly-rate marketplace behind a second **Adviser** tab: a directory of **individuals** per domain — no titled roles ("Legal Lead," "Dev Lead," etc.), just real people — where each one sets `hourly_rate` and a free-text `skills_profile` (what they're good at and why) at onboarding. Advice doesn't price or categorize them beyond that; it applies a `platform_fee_pct` (10% at launch, room to flex within a 10–20% band later) on top at billing time. Round 3's `Verdict` carries both a `price_tier`/budget band and a `real_hours_estimate` range (§6); the matching function multiplies each candidate individual's `hourly_rate` by that range to get an estimated total, filters to whoever lands inside the budget band, scores for fit against both price and the free-text skills profile, and returns a small ranked range (around 3) rather than a single suggestion or an unfiltered browse list. Launch recruiting concentrated in San Francisco for initial supply-side liquidity. On booking, the matched individual is granted access to a generalized **workbench** view pre-loaded with the thread's full Round 1–3 history (context profile, triage reasoning, panel debate and verdict, draft artifact) — the same surface the attorney workbench already provides, generalized to any domain and now also serving as the handoff packet that keeps their real time low. This is the one and only paywall in the whole flow.

## 4. Phases

### Phase 0 — Foundation (mostly unchanged)
Same as `charter-law-roadmap.md` Phase 0: business identity, core accounts, and — for the legal vertical specifically — the reviewing-attorney foundation and compliance structure. Non-legal domains don't need this phase's legal-specific work (no barred professional requirement), but do need a lightweight vetting step before any human expert is allowed to appear in a founder's chat.

### Phase 1 — Manual MVP, generalized
Same idea as before — sell and deliver before building software — but now proven across at least two domains (legal plus one other, pricing is the natural second choice since it's the reference board). Run the triage-then-domain-board process by hand (you playing multiple advisor personas, or Claude prompted manually) before automating it, exactly as the original roadmap ran the legal review by hand first.

### Phase 2 — Landing page + frictionless intake
Build the single-text-box landing page and the submit-then-Clerk-sign-in flow described in §1 steps 1–3. This replaces the old "marketing site with a form" phase — the box itself is the intake, not a separate step.

### Phase 3 — Portal v1, generalized
The existing customer portal work carries over directly: the matter list becomes a thread list, the status tracker becomes a board-progress view (triage → domain board → verdict/delivery), and Clerk's org-based auth is unchanged.

### Phase 4 — Round 1: the context profile + sufficiency check
Build the founder context profile (§3, item 1) and the intake flow that fills it — users, budget, funding stage, traction, customer, team, goals — plus the lighter first use of the board mechanism: a small panel that argues out whether the collected context is enough to fully understand the startup, and if not, generates targeted follow-up questions back to the founder rather than letting a thin profile pass through. This is the simplest version of the board pattern, worth building first since Round 2 and Round 3 both depend on the profile being real and Round 3 depends on the board plumbing this phase proves out.

### Phase 5 — Round 2: problem definition & triage
Build the routing decision: given the context profile and the founder's raw problem description, decide what the actual problem is and which of the seven domains owns it (pricing, fundraising, GTM, pitch, legal, ecosystem, developer/engineering). This is a lighter board than Round 3 — a small visible reasoning step, not the full opposed-positions-plus-Chair mechanism — and it writes the `domain` field that Round 3 reads.

### Phase 6 — Round 3: the domain expert panel (legal first)
The core new build: the board data model, parallel four-expert orchestration, the Chair synthesis step, and the panel's internal `estimated_cost` output (§3, items 2–3) — built first on legal since it plugs into the existing Charter Law pipeline: panel debates template/posture, estimates internally what the matter warrants, feeds playbook-driven AI prep, the attorney workbench, real tracked-changes `.docx` generation (still Priority 2 from the fork super-prompt — unbuilt, still the top legal-side gap), and delivery. The attorney-approval gate is unchanged. Build legal's panel fully before generalizing — it's the shape every other domain's panel will copy.

### Phase 7 — Round 3: remaining domain panels + evidence retrieval
Generalize the artifact pipeline proven in Phase 6 (§3, item 5) to GTM, fundraising, pitch, ecosystem, and developer/engineering: each gets its own template type, its own four-expert panel, and its own review-pass artifact. Add the evidence-retrieval step (§3, item 4) so Round 3 positions are grounded in real, current data rather than model memory.

### Phase 8 — Stage 4: the perfect agent (free) + the individuals marketplace (the one paywall)
Build the persona-synthesis step that turns a completed Round 3 panel into the thread's default sole AI advisor — unlimited, free, no billing hook anywhere in this path. Separately, build the per-domain marketplace of real individuals (§3, item 6; §6): profiles with a self-set `hourly_rate` and `skills_profile`, the budget-band matching function that filters and ranks a domain's individuals against Round 3's `price_tier`, and the top-3 match view the founder chooses from. Wire Stripe billing (with a platform-fee cut, not a set price) to the moment the founder actually books someone — that remains the only point in the entire product where money changes hands.

### Phase 9 — Parallel threads
Let founders run multiple problem threads at once, each reading and updating the one shared context profile from Phase 4 instead of re-running Round 1 from scratch. This is the compounding differentiator: the more threads a founder opens, the less each new one has to ask, and the better every subsequent panel gets.

### Phase 10 — Scale, polish, differentiators
Same spirit as the old Phase 5: voice input/output on the board interface, batch throughput, analytics, and the anonymized market-benchmark data layer — all deferred until real usage justifies the work.

## 5. Invariants (updated)

1. **The attorney gate is unchanged and legal-specific.** No AI or board output reaches a client as a legal deliverable without a barred attorney's approval. This does not extend to other domains, which operate under a different, lighter compliance posture appropriate to non-regulated advice.
2. **Dissent is never smoothed away in Round 3.** The Chair may rule, but a minority position on the domain expert panel that still disagrees must remain visible in the output, not averaged into a single confident answer. This is the core product promise — breaking it quietly turns the panel into a generic chatbot.
3. **Round 3 positions must be genuinely independent before cross-examination.** The domain experts' first drafts cannot see each other's output. Skipping this and simulating disagreement from one shared context is the exact failure mode the reference design calls out — cosmetic disagreement, not structural. Round 2's routing decision is not held to this standard — it's a triage step, not a debate.
4. **Every Round 3 verdict names its own assumptions.** No panel output ships without at least one "if X holds, do A; if it fails, do B" table and a concrete way for the founder to test which one is true. A recommendation with no stated condition for being wrong is not done.
5. **Status transitions, tenant isolation, fail-closed auth, additive migrations, and green CI** — all unchanged from the original Charter Law invariants. Nothing about the pivot loosens these.
6. **Everything through the perfect agent is free, with no exceptions.** Rounds 1–3 and the unlimited Stage 4 AI chat never show a price, a paywall, or a "upgrade to continue" prompt. The only paid action in the product is requesting to be matched with a real individual. Anyone tempted to add usage limits, message caps, or a "free trial" framing to the AI-only path is breaking this — the free layer is the acquisition engine, not a teaser.
7. **The founder never sees Round 3's internal cost estimate as a number until they explicitly ask to be matched.** It exists to price a quote on demand, not to be surfaced as a running total or an upsell nudge during the free AI conversation.
8. **A founder never gets billed past the estimated total shown at booking without explicit sign-off.** Individuals bill hourly underneath, but the founder-facing promise is still cost certainty — the estimate shown for a matched individual functions as a not-to-exceed cap unless the founder approves more hours mid-engagement. This is what keeps an hourly marketplace from quietly reintroducing the open-ended cost risk that flat fees exist to remove (§6).

## 6. Pricing model — hourly-rate marketplace, budget-banded matching, a 10–20% platform fee

Advice doesn't price the work. Individuals price themselves: at recruiting/signup, each one sets their own **hourly rate** and writes a **skills profile** (what they're good at, and why), and Advice takes a **10% platform fee at launch** (with room to move within a 10–20% band later, once volume justifies testing it) on top at billing time — that range is typical of freelance/expert-network marketplaces, but starting flat and low keeps early recruiting simple. Advice's role is matching and the handoff (§3, item 6), not setting a number. Initial recruiting is concentrated in **San Francisco**, so the marketplace has real matching liquidity in one metro before expanding.

What Advice *does* control is the budget band Round 3 targets, anchored to General Legal's own ladder — **$250 / $500 / $1,000 / $2,000** — and, just as importantly, a **real-hours estimate** for the specific job. The band alone can't filter an hourly marketplace; you need hours × rate to know whether a given individual's price actually lands in it. Two questions set both the band and the hours estimate:

1. **Reactive or originative?** Is there something that already exists for the individual to review and mark up (reactive — a contract, an existing GTM motion, an existing pitch deck), or do they have to build the thing from nothing (originative — no plan, no deck, no draft to react to)?
2. **Bounded or open-ended?** Does this end after one review-and-revise pass, or does it require ongoing live back-and-forth (calls, multiple rounds, a negotiation with a third party)?

| Band | Target budget | Real hours estimate | Legal example | Non-legal example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Simple | ~$250 | 10–20 min | NDA, ≤3 pages | A single narrow diagnostic — "here's the one fix" |
| Standard | ~$500 | 45–90 min | 3–25 page contract review + negotiation guide | Reviewing/refining a GTM motion, pricing model, or pitch deck the founder already has |
| Full Negotiation | ~$1,000 | 2–4 hrs cumulative | Unlimited redline rounds + counterparty calls | An existing plan plus ongoing live strategy support as it's executed |
| Drafting | ~$1,000–2,000 | 1.5–3 hrs | ToS, Privacy Policy, MSA, DPA, BAA from nothing | A GTM plan, pricing strategy, or pitch deck built from zero |

Round 3's `Verdict` carries `price_tier`, `real_hours_estimate` (a range), and `estimated_cost` — none of these are shown to the founder as a fixed price. They're inputs to the matching function: `estimated_total = individual.hourly_rate × real_hours_estimate`, filtered to whoever's estimated total lands in the band, ranked, top 3 shown with their rate and estimated total.

### Worked example: GTM for a founder with 20 signed-up customers, none paying

Classified as **originative** (no existing plan to react to) — Drafting band, ~1.5–2.5 hrs real work. Three GTM-savvy individuals on the platform might price out like this:

| Individual | Hourly rate | Estimated hours | Estimated total (before fee) | + 15% platform fee |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | $250/hr | 1.5–2 hrs | $375–500 | $430–575 |
| B | $400/hr | 1.5–2 hrs | $600–800 | $690–920 |
| C | $600/hr | 2–2.5 hrs | $1,200–1,500 | $1,380–1,725 |

All three could plausibly surface depending on how tightly the band is enforced — this is where the model gets honest: **an hourly marketplace won't cleanly reproduce the flat, value-based $1,000 quote from the earlier General Legal-style analysis.** A junior or newly-onboarded individual pricing at $250/hr looks like a bargain next to a seasoned operator at $600/hr, even though the earlier reasoning ("this is origination work, worth Drafting-tier money regardless of whose hours it takes") argued the *outcome* is worth ~$1,000 either way. That's a real, known tension between hourly billing (transparent, standard for a two-sided marketplace, easy for individuals to trust) and value-based fixed pricing (what McKinsey/BCG/Bain deliberately use instead, because hourly billing lets the client optimize for fewer hours instead of a better outcome, and reopens the cost-risk that fixed fees exist to remove). Two ways to soften it, worth deciding rather than ignoring: **(a)** show the founder the estimated total as a **not-to-exceed cap** at booking — the individual can bill less if the job goes faster, but needs the founder's explicit sign-off to bill past the estimate, which keeps the cost-certainty promise even though billing is hourly underneath; **(b)** let Round 3's fit-ranking weight *value*, not just budget-band membership, so a highly-rated $600/hr individual can still outrank a $250/hr one when the panel's verdict suggests this problem specifically rewards seniority (e.g. Full Negotiation-band problems with real counterparty stakes) — cheapest-that-fits isn't always the right match.

This is the reference case for how Round 3's classifier should reason about any problem in any of the seven domains: ask whether it's reactive or originative and bounded or open-ended first, use that to set the band and hours estimate, and let each individual's own hourly rate — filtered and ranked against that estimate, not set by Advice — decide what the founder actually pays.

## 7. Open questions

1. Which domain's panel ships second, after legal — pricing (matches the reference build most closely) or developer/engineering (closer to your own network for sourcing individuals)?
2. What counts as "board-worthy" versus a normal single-advisor chat — every question, or only ones the triage board itself flags as high-stakes/contested? (Recommend: let the triage board decide per-thread rather than hard-coding a list.)
3. **Resolved this session:** sourcing is a genuine self-serve marketplace of real individuals — no titled roles, anyone can join a domain, set their own hourly rate, and get matched. Worth revisiting whether legal needs an exception given attorney vetting/bar requirements (§ Phase 0), since "anyone can join and set a rate" can't apply to the attorney role the same way it applies to a pricing or GTM individual.
4. Does legal's attorney engagement still route through this same hourly-rate marketplace mechanism once a founder requests to be matched, or does the malpractice-insurance/bar-compliance overhead mean legal needs a smaller, pre-vetted pool rather than an open self-serve join flow the other six domains can use?
5. How is "developer/engineering" scoped so it doesn't become an unbounded software-dev-shop offering — e.g. limited to advisory (architecture review, build-vs-buy, hiring plan) rather than Advice actually writing the founder's product code?
6. **Not-to-exceed enforcement (§5, invariant 8):** is the cap hard (an individual literally cannot bill past the estimate without a new approval flow) or soft (a policy Advice enforces via dispute resolution after the fact)? Hard is safer for the founder-trust promise but adds real engineering scope to Phase 8.
7. Value-weighted matching vs. cheapest-that-fits (§6): should Round 3's fit score ever let a higher-priced, more senior individual outrank a cheaper one within the same budget band, or does "top 3 within budget" always mean strictly filtering by price first?
