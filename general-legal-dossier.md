They don't sell software — they sell **the legal work done**, with a **U.S.-barred attorney owning every deliverable** and AI compressing the routine first pass. Wedge product: **flat-fee commercial contract review**, turned in **hours not days**, delivered over **Slack, email, or a client portal**.

## 2. Company & business model

**The thesis:** clients don't want another legal tool, they want the legal work done. The "law firm + AI vendor" path is too slow; instead, build a **law firm where AI is in the attorney workflow from the ground up.** AI does the routine first pass; a barred attorney applies judgment and **signs off with their name, reputation, and bar license** ("No AI slop included").

**Why "law firm" not "software"** — this is the strategic core: because a licensed attorney reviews and owns every output, they can practise law (and carry malpractice/professional-responsibility obligations) rather than being a tech vendor. This is also their **UPL (unauthorized practice of law) compliance mechanism** — the human attorney as final reviewer is what legally lets them operate as a firm.

**Positioning line:** *"Outside counsel that scales like software."* / *"Lightning fast, Biglaw quality legal for business."*

**Practice areas (as of June 2026):** Technology Transactions · Employment · Data Privacy · Financial Regulatory · Blockchain & Cryptocurrency. (Plus emerging companies / fundraising docs like SAFEs and side letters.)

**Jurisdiction:** supports companies doing business in **all U.S. states**. **Hours:** 5:00 AM–6:00 PM PT, Mon–Fri, excl. federal holidays. **Guarantee:** initial response + first turn within **3 hours** during operating hours.

## 7. The operating workflow (how the service actually runs)

This is the part most worth copying structurally. From their own CEO blog post + site:

1. **Intake** — client sends a contract over **Slack** (most common), email, or the **Client Portal**. Each client gets a **private Slack channel monitored by bots** that detect new requests, trigger AI workflows, and schedule attorney time.
2. **Scope/triage** — an attorney (or Triage Attorney) confirms scope; flat-fee quote given.
3. **Sentinel AI first pass (~10 seconds)** — their contract AI produces **redlines, annotations, and an issues list**, optionally guided by an **attorney-defined strategy**. Deliberately **over-inclusive** (high recall).
4. **Attorney review (15–30 min)** — attorney reviews the full contract + the AI markup **inside Microsoft Word only**, using their **custom Word add-in** for extra AI help without leaving Word. Full client context (including prior contracts) is in AI memory and accessible to the attorney.
5. **Deliver** — final **`*_redline.docx`** with tracked changes + comments, returned wherever the client sent it.

**Status pipeline (confirmed via MCP + site):**
`intake → ai_review → attorney_queue → attorney_review → delivered → completed`

**Sentinel's design philosophy (their 4 premises):**
1. LLMs are good at issue detection, redlining, annotation.
2. LLM agents are bad at interpreting client needs, leverage, deal strategy.
3. LLMs don't know "what's market" and are overconfident + over-aggressive in mark-ups.
4. Great attorneys are strong exactly where LLMs are weak (and often weaker on raw markup).
→ So **AI does the high-recall first pass; the attorney supplies judgment, market-awareness, and strategy.** They cite **~80% of work by AI**, **5–10x leverage per attorney**, trending toward **100x**.

**Non-AI accelerators they stress:** everything happens **in Word only** (no tool-switching); great UX for attorney tools; **bots/agents automate every handoff** (scheduling, notifications) to kill the email-driven dead time of traditional firms.

## 9. The MCP server (full spec — they were first)

- **Endpoint:** `https://mcp.general.legal/mcp` · **Transport:** Streamable HTTP · **Auth:** OAuth 2.1 with automatic discovery (no API keys).
- **Connect (Claude Code):** `claude mcp add "general-legal" https://mcp.general.legal/mcp`
- **Four tools:** `upload_contract` (returns signed upload URL) · `confirm_upload` (triggers pipeline, optional context) · `list_contracts` (status) · `download_contract` (when `delivered`).
- **Files:** `.docx` only; returns tracked changes + attorney comments.
- **Positioning:** the **first production MCP server operated by an actual law firm** — output is attorney-reviewed legal work, not AI advice. They also publicly staged being **"the first law firm hired by an AI agent"** (via rentahuman.ai).

## 12. Implications for Charter Law (copy the structure, not the content)

**Copy (the proven playbook):**
- The **attorney-owns-the-output** model as the legal/UPL backbone and the core marketing promise.
- The **status pipeline** (`intake → ai_review → attorney_queue → attorney_review → delivered → completed`) — adopt verbatim; it's a clean spec.
- **Flat-fee tiers** anchored near $250/$500/$1,000/$2,000.
- **Work-where-the-client-works** intake (Slack/email/portal) and **everything-in-Word** delivery with a Word add-in for the reviewer.
- **AI = high-recall first pass, attorney = judgment/market/strategy.** Don't let AI output reach the client.
- The **stack** (Webflow → React portal → FastAPI/Postgres → Clerk → Stripe → Cloudflare → Claude), with **Cloud Run** instead of their App Engine.
- Later: an **MCP server** and **free template library / a viral free tool** as top-of-funnel.

**Do differently (our context: solo founder, per-job attorney):**
- We can't field 11 attorneys — start with **one per-matter reviewing attorney** and a **narrow practice area** (commercial contracts).
- Lean harder on **off-the-shelf** (Stripe hosted checkout, the Claude Word add-in rather than a bespoke one) to keep the solo build feasible.
- **Don't copy** their copy, brand, templates, or layout code — build our own.

## E. Operating model — the fuller 14-step reconstruction
§7 above captured the core loop; the Standard Legal memo reconstructs 14 steps. The extra operational levers worth internalizing:
- Bots turn each Slack message into a tracked **matter** (not a passive inbox item) — explicit queues you can measure, route, and price.
- **Intake / scope / engagement checks:** a **standard lane vs an exception lane** (known client, supported doc type/jurisdiction, normal page count → proceed; unusual / high-stakes / out-of-scope → escalate). This is what protects flat-fee predictability.
- **Attorney queue & shift coverage:** pool attorney capacity and route prepared matters to the next qualified attorney in a coverage window — attorney time is the scarce input, so if AI takes 10s but the queue takes hours, the bottleneck is staffing.
- **QC inside the workflow** (playbooks, checklists, client-preference memory, sampling), not after delivery.
- **Follow-up turns reuse context** (prior contracts, redlines, posture held in AI memory) so turn 2 is faster than turn 1 — this is how the wedge expands inside a trusted channel.
- **Metrics as the operating dashboard:** throughput, cycle time (submission→delivery, not just working time), variance, staffing, escalation rate, rework rate, margin per matter, CSAT.
- **Continuous improvement:** every completed matter tunes prompts, playbooks, the clause library, and pricing.
- Ryan Walker's formula: **"sophisticated contract AI + boring operational technology + talent density."** The *boring ops tech* (routing, scheduling, notifications, status) matters as much as the AI.

## J. Corporate structure — the "Atrium model" (most important single find for compliance)
- Per the agents.law interview, General Legal's structure **mirrors Atrium**: a **separate law firm entity** that employs the attorneys, **plus a partner technology company** that builds the software and runs operations. The two-entity split is how they **sidestep ABS / Rule 5.4** limits on non-lawyer ownership and fee-sharing — the tech co can take venture capital and own the IP; the firm practises law.
- Philosophy: **reinvest ~all profits into efficiency** rather than distributing them to partners. They claim traditional firms spend only **1–2% of profits** on efficiency tooling — a structural reason incumbents can't keep pace.
- → **For Charter Law:** this is the concrete, copyable compliance architecture — a lawyer-owned firm/PC + your operating/tech company — but it MUST be set up by a real lawyer for your jurisdiction. Treat as a model to take to counsel, not as legal advice. (Now reflected in the roadmap's Phase 0.)

## K. Full pricing schedule + the pricing lesson they learned
- **Employment:** **$250** (offer letters, NDAs/IP assignment, arbitration, policies, wage/hour counseling); **$500** (commission/bonus plans, IC drafting, leave/accommodation, restrictive covenants, terminations, separation agreements); **$1,500** executive employment agreements; **$2,500** (handbooks, misclassification audits, RIF/restructuring, equity incentive plans); **$3,500** full executive separations.
- **ECVC:** **$250** advisor-agreement / SAFE drafts; **$500** side-letter draft; **$1,000** negotiation tier (advisor/SAFE/side letter each); **$2,000** foundational drafting (TOS/Privacy/MSA/DPA/BAA); **$1,000** basic research memos.
- **The lesson (copy this):** they found **most clients don't actually want negotiation — they want a clean redline plus a clear explanation of what changed and why**, then they close the deal themselves. So **$500 standard** now = redline + one revision round + a **"negotiation-guide" cover letter** (every change, the risk of the original language, a fallback position); **$1,000** = full negotiation (unlimited turns + counterparty calls). *"Most of our clients were paying for negotiation muscle they didn't need."*
- **GC-as-a-service** subscription is in pilot: unlimited work, priced by company size/stage.

## L. ICP (sharpened) and the land-and-expand motion
- **ICP:** growth-stage (~**Series A–B**) companies — enough contract volume to hurt, too small to justify a full-time GC. **Not** pre-seed, **not** Fortune 500 with in-house counsel. Vivid pain they sell to: "almost lost a $400K deal because counsel took 3 weeks on one MSA"; "the 60-page customer paper that arrives at 4:45pm on a Friday."
- **Expansion is client pull, not push:** they launched Employment + ECVC because existing clients kept asking adjacent questions *in the same Slack channel*. Win trust on one repeatable workflow → expand horizontally into the practices a growth-stage company hits constantly. "Once a founder gets a sales contract turned in three hours over Slack, they don't want to email a different firm for an offer letter."

## M. The human/AI division of labour — concrete (from "Balancing Attorney and Machine")
- AI (Sentinel) is excellent at issue-spotting — in their test it **surpassed** the attorney on economic-asymmetry detection (caught a liability-cap arbitrage worth ~$0.5M). But the **attorney makes the decisions**: rewrote an overbroad 10-yr non-compete into an enforceable "Exclusivity" article; knew an "Inspection & Supervision" clause shouldn't be a default because of how it's *used and fought over* in practice. **AI carries a sycophancy / pattern-matching bias** (it accepts the patterns it sees). The line to remember: *"The AI told me what was wrong. The attorney decided what to do about it."*
- The % of AI vs human is **matter-dependent**, not fixed: a DPA draft might be ~90% AI; GDPR-compliance advice ~98% human.

## N. Strategic philosophy (from "We build for clients")
- Being a **firm that sells outcomes** rather than a **tools vendor** means foundation-model progress (e.g. Anthropic's Word plugin) **helps** them — they're **tool-agnostic** and just adopt the best tool available. A tools vendor would be *threatened* by the very same release. (They even said they'd likely integrate Anthropic's Word plugin into their workflow.)
- Mission/TAM framing: push price down into the **"92% of unmet legal demand in the US."**

## O. Moat & risks (third-party StartupHub read)
- **Moats:** law-firm **licensure** (regulatory — "you cannot vibe-code your way to a bar license"); proprietary **playbook/redline data that compounds with every matter**; **team credibility** (Casetext/CoCounsel); **attorney trust + switching costs** (they hold your playbooks and negotiation history).
- **Build-difficulty read:** Frontend **2/10** (it lives in Slack), Backend 5, ML/AI 7, Data 8, DevOps 4 — *"the non-technical barriers are higher than the technical ones."* Encouraging for a solo builder: the hard parts here are legal/operational, not front-end engineering.
- **Bear vs bull:** bear = if AI gets 10× better, the human-review premium compresses toward "a dev wrapping an API in a Slack bot." Bull = better AI just lowers COGS while the regulatory moat holds, **widening** the price gap vs traditional firms. The founders bet bull.
- General Legal is explicitly framed as a **template for AI-native professional services** (accounting, HR compliance, financial advisory next).

## P. The YC "AI-native services" playbook
Captured in full in the new **`charter-law-operating-playbook.md`**. Headlines: pick markets with **low trust, low task-level judgment, high intelligence threshold, and helpful regulation**; pass the **"Sam Altman test"** (better models must strengthen you, not commoditise you); **variance is the existential risk** (customers fire you for inconsistency faster than for slow/expensive); beware the **early-demand trap** (cap your first pilots); **sell outcomes, price on value**; obsess over **COGS and AI operating leverage**; and **build, don't buy.**
