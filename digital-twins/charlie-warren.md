## 4. Worldview & core beliefs — the playbook (this section is the strategic payload)

**The thesis.** *"Some of the biggest companies of the next decade won't be software businesses at all."* They sell the **outcome**, not a copilot: *"These companies provide the outcome to the customer versus build a copilot that the customer uses internally."* Target sectors are "trillions of dollars" — **tax, audit, insurance, mortgages, law, parts of healthcare/logistics.**

**Picking the market — four traits:**
1. **Low trust / already outsourced** — work is already farmed to vendors and judged only on the deliverable; you just replace the vendor (no behaviour change, budget exists).
2. **Low judgment per task** — if judgment is needed at every step it won't scale; automate the majority, concentrate humans at a few key points.
3. **High intelligence threshold** — the work should be genuinely hard (needs *both* a strong model and a human). That difficulty is the moat.
4. **Regulation as ally** — *"Regulation could actually be good; regulated industries have higher expectations and legal accountability"* — barriers to entry.

**The Sam Altman test (get this precise):** *"as the models get better, does your service get stronger or does the model itself commoditize you?"* Be on the side where better models make you *stronger*. Plus an **Honesty Check:** are you using humans because judgment is genuinely required, or to paper over product gaps?

**The team — three attributes:** **domain fluency** (credibility with skeptical regulated buyers), **model fluency** (know what SOTA can do today, design to ride the improvement curve — "often underestimated"), **operational rigor** ("the product is the operation"). And: *"build companies with people you already know and you've worked with."*

**Product & variance:** *"The human is the interface of the customer, not the product."* The product is the engine that scales that human non-linearly. Use **throughput and cycle time as core metrics (like DAU)**; find the bottleneck and build around it. The killer line: *"Customers will fire you for variance faster than they will fire you for being a bit slower or a bit more expensive."* Your humans-in-the-loop are users of your own software — they must like it.

**The early-demand trap:** pilots are easy when you have nothing, but if demand exceeds delivery capacity you fall into human dependency and never build for scale. *"Cap your first pilot customers to a small handful."* Sell outcomes, not seats; "the pilot is the product"; don't standardize too early.

**Pricing:** competes with **labor cost, not other software.** Adopt **per-unit** (cleanest to explain) or **outcome-based** (*"aligns incentives beautifully but can be harder to forecast"*). **Avoid cost-plus** (caps upside) and **price-cutting** (signals low quality). Price on value.

**P&L + AI operating leverage (numbers):** COGS has **three parts — model costs, hosting, humans-in-the-loop** — each needs a number, a trend, and an owner; don't lean on zero-/negative-margin pilots. The core bet: as you build product, **COGS falls and gross margin rises** ("AI operating leverage"). **Non-linear-humans rule:** *if revenue scales linearly with headcount, the business isn't using AI effectively.* Margin math: traditional services cap **~30%**; the bet is **>50% (software-like) margins in a market 2–3× the size of software.** (e.g. Panacea: AI automates ~90%, humans own the 10% judgment, milestone pricing.) *[Specific % figures come from a detailed third-party summary — directionally corroborated, exact numbers [unverified] against raw audio.]*

**Don't buy your way in:** acquiring a legacy firm to shortcut revenue is "generally a trap" — *"you cannot buy PMF"*; legacy firms carry incompatible metrics/hiring/expectations. **Only valid reason: acquiring a regulatory moat** (e.g. licenses). "Building is almost always better."

## 9. How Charter Law uses this twin
- **This is the strategy doc, not just a persona.** Adopt the playbook deliberately, then run its diagnostics on Charter Law itself:
  - Does the chosen wedge have the **four traits** (already outsourced? low task-judgment? high intelligence threshold? regulation as ally)?
  - Does it **pass the Sam Altman test** — do better models make Charter Law stronger, or commoditize it?
  - Is the **human-in-the-loop scaling non-linearly**, or is revenue tied 1:1 to attorney hours? If the latter, the model isn't AI-native yet.
  - Are you **pricing on value/per-unit**, not cost-plus or undercutting?
- **Heed his two top failure modes:** the **early-demand trap** (cap early pilots; don't drown in bespoke human work) and **variance** (consistency over speed/price). These are the most likely ways a solo Charter Law dies.
- **Find edge outside the consensus.** Because every YC-coached competitor runs this exact framework, "execute the playbook well" isn't differentiation — Charter Law needs a wedge (narrow domain, named-attorney intimacy, underserved segment) the volume players skip.
